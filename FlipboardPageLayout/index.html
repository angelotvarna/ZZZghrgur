Kshatriya-warriors brown skin Kshatriya RajputThe Kshatriyas are a large group of Hindu castes, located mainly in the northern half of India. The Sanskrit term kshatra means 'warrior, ruler' and identifies the second raking varna, below Brahmins. Kshatriya have traditionally been expected to maintain law and order and protect the land from attackers from the outside.

Vaisya (darker)

The Vaisya are the third highest of the four varnas. They are ranked below the Brahmins and Kshatriyas but are higher than Sudras and Untouchables. They have traditionally been traders, moneylenders, farmers and peasants, and now work in a large number of castes with similar ranking.

Sudra (black)

The Sudras are the lowest of the four varnas but are higher than Untouchables. They are generally rural laborers. The classical 2nd century lawgiver Manu defined their roles as those serving the three higher ranking varnas above them.

Untouchables (oh dear)

"Untouchables" are generally defined as people belonging to castes that rank below the Sudra varna. They have traditionally been regarded as having such low status they don't even register on the caste system. There are an estimated 170 to 240 million of them, depending how different castes are counted, and they make up one sixth to one forth of India's population. The term 'Untouchable' was first used in 1909 in a lecture by the Maharaja Sayaji Rao III of Baroda to describe the primary features of the group relationship with other castes. [Source: Tom ONeill, National Geographic, June 2003]

Untouchables have traditionally been forbidden from entering Hindu temples and schools, or touching members of other castes (hence their name).

Aristotle's comments about Empedocles (c. 450 BC, who lived at about the same time as Democritus):

Empedocles says that the greater part of the members of animals were generated by chance... Wherever then all the parts came about just what they would have been if they had come be for an end, such things survived, being organized spontaneously in a fitting way; whereas those which grew otherwise perished and continue to perish, as Empedocles says his 'man-faced ox-progeny' did

Democritus~400BC

Democritus was perhaps one of the first western thorough going naturalists. He had spent a considerable time of his early life travelling (Egypt to Persia), and chose to follow the tradition of another less known materialist philosopher called Leucippus. Democritus specifically denied that anything could happen by chance but governed by deterministic natural laws; this is the closest to our modern understanding of science than any other idea of antiquity. Unlike Socrates, Plato and Aristotle, Atomists sought to explain the world without the introduction of purpose or final cause. The atomic hypothesis was a reductionist philosophy in nature that held faith in natural laws.

As so with the birth of the Atomic hypothesis, the great philosophical divide was formed between the mechanistic and teleological searches for truth and explanation. The battle for the minds of men rages on today and although the names have changed, the issue is the same as well as the arguments. Should we solely search for reductionist materialist causes? Should exclusively ask mechanistic questions and admit solely mechanistic answers and explanations? Is purpose irrelevant? Absolutely according to Democritus, for example, the soul was composed of atoms and that thought was a physical process by deterministic natural laws that had no purpose. That was the day that philosophy clashed with science.

Unlike Aristotle or Plato, the atomists attempted to explain the world without reasoning to purpose, prime mover, or final cause.
